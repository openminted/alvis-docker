AGL15 is an Arabidopsis thaliana MADS-domain regulatory factor that not only preferentially accumulates during embryogenesis but is also expressed at lower levels after the completion of germination. To better understand the control of expression of AGL15, a series of 5′ and internal deletions within the regulatory regions of AGL15 was generated. Regions important for the level of expression, including a region involved in expression in response to auxin, were identified. Additionally, AGL15 expression was found to respond to AGL15 accumulation amounts and to altered forms of AGL15. This feedback loop is at least in part due to direct regulation, as assessed by in vivo and in vitro binding of AGL15 to its own regulatory regions and by site-directed mutagenesis studies.

MADS-box genes encode a large family of transcriptional regulators present in fungi, plants and animals, the acronym derived from four of the first gene products found to contain the conserved 55–60 amino acid residue domain termed the MADS-domain (reviewed in Riechmann and Meyerowitz, 1997). The MADS-domain is involved in sequence-specific binding to DNA and in dimer formation, either homodimers, or heterodimers of two members of this family. MADS-domain proteins bind cis elements with a canonical sequence of CC[A/T]6GG termed a CArG motif (for C-A/T-rich-G), but considerable variation in sequences recognized exists. Over 100 genes encoding MADS-domain proteins are present in Arabidopsis thaliana and are subgrouped based on structural features (Parenicováet al., 2003). The MIKC subgroup, consisting of 39 members in Arabidopsis, shares not only the MADS-domain, but also has a second, more weakly conserved domain termed the K-domain in recognition of shared structural features with keratin (reviewed in Irish, 2003). A short linker, the I-domain, is present between the MADS- and K-domains. The C-terminal domain is quite divergent and is made up of a transcriptional activation domain in some MADS-domain proteins (Cho et al., 1999), while in others it appears to be involved in protein–protein interactions (Egea-Cortines et al., 1999; Honma and Goto, 2001). In most cases, however, the function of the C-terminal domain remains unidentified.

The best understood members of the plant MADS-box family are expressed during reproductive development and are involved in control of flowering time, meristem and floral organ identity, and in fruit development (recent reviews include Irish, 2003; Ng and Yanofsky, 2001; Theißen, 2001; Zik and Irish, 2003). Interactions between these genes and their products occur at both the transcriptional and post-translational levels, with examples of autoregulation, cross-regulation and complex formation reported (Egea-Cortines et al., 1999; Goto and Meyerowitz, 1994; Hill et al., 1998; Honma and Goto, 2001; Lamb et al., 2002; McGonigle et al., 1996; Tilly et al., 1998). Other family members are preferentially expressed in other developmental contexts including leaves, roots and embryos (Alvarez-Buylla et al., 2000; Burgeff et al., 2002; Heck et al., 1995; Rounsley et al., 1995; Zhang and Forde, 1998). Much less is understood about how these genes function. AGL15 is the only member of the MIKC subgroup identified to date that is preferentially expressed during embryo development, and this gene product accumulates in nuclei of cells as early as the octant stage, persisting through maturation stage, and showing decreased expression/accumulation during desiccation (Heck et al., 1995; Perry et al., 1996; Rounsley et al., 1995). Proteins that react with AGL15-specific antiserum, and may represent orthologs, have been found to accumulate in a wide variety of angiosperm embryonic tissues (Perry et al., 1996, 1999). Constitutive expression of AGL15 supports long-term maintenance of embryogenic development of tissue derived from cultured zygotic embryos, and promotes somatic embryo formation after the completion of germination from the shoot apical meristem of seedlings in culture (Harding et al., 2003). However, unlike other transcriptional regulators that promote embryo development when ectopically expressed (e.g., LEC1, LEC2; Lotan et al., 1998; Stone et al., 2001), AGL15 is not exclusively expressed during the embryonic phase of development. AGL15 is also expressed at lower levels after completion of germination in restricted sets of cells including the vegetative shoot apical meristem, leaf primordia, young flower buds, and in the base of expanding lateral organs (rosette and cauline leaves, and floral organs; Fernandez et al., 2000). Prolonged and higher level expression leads to delay in abscission and/or senescence in reproductive tissues, indicating that the normally transient expression from the native promoter may also be involved in these processes (Fang and Fernandez, 2002; Fernandez et al., 2000).

Global expression of developmental regulators at relatively high levels, followed by restricted low-level expression, is not uncommon and may represent situations where a gene was co-opted to perform a new function as systems became increasingly complex (DeKoter and Singh, 2000; Emambokus et al., 2003; Imai et al., 2002; Miklos and Rubin, 1996). Identification of cis elements controlling particular aspects of temporal and spatial expression should provide insight into gene function and factors involved in the control of expression. To better understand the regulation of AGL15 expression, we dissected AGL15 regulatory regions to identify cis elements important for particular expression features using reporter constructs (Jefferson et al., 1987). Our results show that some DNA sequences were involved in aspects of tissue-specific expression patterns, while other regions appeared to control AGL15 expression level. Interestingly, we found that one fragment conferred expression of AGL15 in response to auxin. Furthermore, we show that AGL15 could bind to DNA fragments including potential CArG motifs within the 5′ regulatory region and control its own expression. Site-directed mutagenesis confirmed that at least two potential binding sites for AGL15 are involved in the control of expression.

In order to better define regulatory regions important for correct temporal and spatial expression of AGL15, a series of deletion constructs was generated. An initial construct consisting of 1260 bp 5′ of the start codon of AGL15, the first four codons of AGL15 translationally fused to the coding region of the β-glucuronidase (uidA, GUS) gene, and 524 bp 3′ of the stop codon of AGL15 was generated (Figure 1a, construct 1260; for the sake of simplicity, constructs are named according to the length of the 5′ regulatory region). The length of the 5′ and 3′ regions included in this construct was defined by identifying the intergenic regions to the coding sequence of AGL15 (At5g13790) in the Arabidopsis genome sequence database (The Arabidopsis Initiative, 2000). Expressed sequence tags for both predicted flanking genes (At5g13780 and At5g13800) are present in the database, suggesting that they are not pseudogenes. Construct 1260 was stably introduced into Arabidopsis ecotype Ws plants, independent transgenic lines obtained, and GUS activity assessed. Expression of 1260 was the same as that of previously reported transformants harboring an AGL15:GUS reporter construct that consisted of approximately 2.5 kb 5′ of the start codon, the first four codons of AGL15, and approximately 2.5 kb 3′ of the stop codon (Fernandez et al., 2000, and data not shown). When the 524 bp sequence corresponding to the 3′ region downstream of the AGL15 stop codon was deleted (1260Δ3′), the expression level of GUS was significantly decreased to less than half of that of 1260 as assessed using 4-methylumbelliferyl-β-d-glucuronide (MUG) assays (Gallagher, 1992), although the pattern of GUS accumulation was the same as for 1260 (Figure 1b and data not shown). All other constructs included the 3′ region. Thus, the 1260 bp 5′ and 524 bp 3′ to the coding region for AGL15 encompassed all the regulatory elements important for correct temporal and spatial expression of AGL15 and could serve as a starting point for the identification of regions containing essential cis elements.

A series of GUS reporter constructs that contained different portions of the 5′ regulatory regions of AGL15 was generated and stably introduced into Arabidopsis to identify cis elements important for the control of AGL15 expression (Figure 1a). The expression pattern of each construct was assessed using GUS activity both qualitatively in various tissues (data not shown) and quantitatively in 10-day-old seedlings of 18–25 independent transgenic lines (Figure 1b), identifying regions with positive or negative effects on the expression of the reporter constructs (Figure 1b and data not shown). For example, the GUS activity of seedlings harboring the 1158 transgene (deletion to −1158 bp, Figure 1a) was approximately one-third of that of 1260 seedlings. However, deletion of the 5′ region to −1056 bp increased GUS activity to nearly 1260 levels and further deletion to −956 bp significantly increased the level of GUS activity above that found in 1260. Deletion of the 5′ region to −859 of the start codon decreased GUS activity to background levels in seedlings (Figure 1b, compare to Ws, wild-type untransformed seedlings) and in other tissues with the exception of low-level staining observed in developing pollen (data not shown). Likewise, no GUS activity, with the exception of developing pollen, was observed upon further deletion to −444.

To examine potential elements within the 5′ region downstream of −860, a series of internal deletion constructs was created. All internal deletion constructs contained the −1260 to −860 region to ensure that the necessary elements for expression of AGL15 were retained. An internal deletion of −859 through −756 within the −1260 5′ region (construct Δ859-756, Figure 1a) did not significantly change the expression level of the reporter gene compared with 1260 (Figure 1b). A larger deletion encompassing −859 to −639 significantly increased the expression level to about 2.3-fold than that observed in 1260 transgenic seedlings (Figure 1b). Further internal deletions toward the translational start codon of AGL15 first decreased GUS activity to a level similar to that found for 1260 (Figure 1a,b; Δ859-383 and Δ859-303) and then dramatically increased expression (Δ859-207 and Δ859-128, Figure 1a,b).

In general, transgenes within the deletion series that showed GUS activity also showed a similar spatial pattern of staining as found for 1260 and as previously reported (Fernandez et al., 2000). GUS activity from transgene 1260 was detected in the developing embryo (Figure 2a,b) and declined as the embryo matured, finally disappearing from the shoot apical meristem region (Figure 2c and data not shown). GUS activity was also observed in the seedling shoot apical meristem (SAM, data not shown), and at some stages of flower development, predominantly in the abscission zone (Figure 2d). Like 1260 (Figure 2d) and the previously reported expression pattern for AGL15:GUS (Fernandez et al., 2000), for most constructs that exhibited GUS expression, no GUS activity was detected at the base of the floral organs by the time of anthesis (data not shown). However, transgenic plants containing some constructs within the series showed some dramatic changes in the pattern of GUS activity in reproductive tissues. Plants containing transgenes Δ859-207 and Δ859-128 not only showed increased GUS activity in the SAM of seedlings (Figure 1b), but also prolonged expression in the abscission zones, in open flowers (Figure 2f) and even as late as nearly mature siliques (Figure 2e, compare to 1260, Figure 2d).

Generally, treatment with exogenous auxin is important for the induction of somatic embryogenesis (recent reviews include von Arnold et al., 2002; Feher et al., 2003). Because AGL15 accumulates in a wide variety of embryonic tissues, including somatic embryos (Perry et al., 1999), we examined the regulatory regions of AGL15 for elements involved in auxin response. A sequence similar to that corresponding to part of the NDE element (Nde I containing sequence) from the soybean small auxin-up RNA (SAUR) 15A gene promoter that is involved in auxin response (Xu et al., 1997) was found −1050 bp 5′ of the ATG start codon of AGL15. To determine whether the regulatory regions of AGL15 respond to the addition of exogenous auxin, and whether the NDE-like element may be involved in this response, transgenic lines with constructs 1260 (includes the partial NDE sequence) and 956 (lacks the partial NDE sequence) were assessed for auxin response by treating 8-day transgenic seedlings with the synthetic auxin 2,4-D for two days. As shown in Figure 3(a), GUS activity was significantly increased in the auxin-treated samples compared with the non-treated controls for transgenic lines carrying 1260. However, the increase in GUS activity in response to exogenous auxin was much lower for transgenic lines carrying construct 956 and was not significantly different from untreated samples (Figure 3a). Additionally, semiquantitative RT-PCR was performed to determine the accumulation of endogenous AGL15 and transgenic GUS transcripts in response to exogenous auxin in 1260 and 956 plants (Figure 3b). The increased accumulation of GUS transcript with 2,4-D treatment was higher in 1260-seedlings than in 956-seedlings, while the native AGL15 transcript in both transgenic lines showed a similar induction of expression in response to 2,4-D (Figure 3b).

Because MADS-domain proteins regulate their own expression and control the expression of other MADS-box genes (Hill et al., 1998; Jack et al., 1994; Lamb et al., 2002; Tilly et al., 1998), AGL15 was evaluated for potential feedback control of expression. AGL15:GUS reporter constructs were introduced into backgrounds where AGL15 (35S:AGL15) was constitutively expressed. A transgenic line containing a single homozygous insert of 1260 was used for crosses to plants carrying a hemizygous copy of the 35S:AGL15 transgene. The F1 seedlings from these crosses segregated 1:1 for the presence or absence of the 35S:AGL15 transgene and could be separated at the seedling stage (Fernandez et al., 2000; referred to as overexpressor siblings and wild-type siblings, respectively). As shown in Figure 4(a), decreased GUS activity was observed in siblings carrying the 35S:AGL15 transgene compared with siblings expressing AGL15 only from the endogenous gene. MUG assays confirmed that GUS activity in 10-day-old seedlings constitutively expressing AGL15 was approximately 20% of the level of activity found in wild-type siblings (Figure 4b). Conversely, when the 1260 transgene was introduced into agl15 plants, GUS activity was significantly increased to about 1.5-fold compared with wild type (Figure 4c). agl15 plants contained a T-DNA insertion in the first intron, and no correctly spliced full-length transcript could be detected by RT-PCR (M.D. Lehti-Shiu and D.E. Fernandez, University of Wisconsin-Madison, Madison, WI, USA, personal communication).

To further test for regulation of AGL15 by its own gene product, we examined whether AGL15 expression responds to modified forms of AGL15. AGL15:GUS (1260) was crossed with a transgenic line that constitutively expressed a modified form of AGL15 that included a strong transcriptional activation domain (Sadowski et al., 1988; 35S:AGL15-VP16). The 35S:AGL15-VP16 transgene caused tight upward curling of cotyledons and rosette leaves. In the F1 segregating population, GUS activity was increased and present in a broader domain in the AGL15-VP16 siblings compared with wild-type siblings (Figure 4d).

Expression of AGL15:GUS in backgrounds accumulating different amounts and forms of AGL15 indicated the existence of a negative feedback loop leading to a net repression of expression. However, autoregulation may be direct or indirect. MADS-domain proteins bind DNA sequences referred to as CArG motifs that have a canonical sequence of CC(A/T)6GG, (reviewed in Riechmann and Meyerowitz, 1997). No canonical CArG motifs were identified in the 5′ regulatory region of AGL15, but several non-canonical CArG motifs are present, including two of form C(A/T)8G that AGL15 preferentially binds in vitro (Tang and Perry, 2003). These two potential cis elements are located at −1198 to −1189 and −671 to −662 and referred to as CArG1 and CArG2, respectively. A third potential binding site, predicted using MatInspector (http://www.genomatix.de), is located at −279 to −270 (CArG3) and has the form CC(A/T)4NNGG. Both AGAMOUS (Shiraishi et al., 1993) and AGL15 (Tang and Perry, 2003) can bind this type of DNA sequence in vitro.

To test whether AGL15 binds directly to its own promoter, enrichment assays were performed. This assay investigates whether a given DNA fragment is represented at higher levels in a DNA population selected by chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP) than in the total input DNA population, relative to a non-selected control. AGL15-specific antiserum was used to select in vivo-formed AGL15-DNA complexes from an embryonic tissue culture constitutively expressing AGL15 (Harding et al., 2003). The co-precipitated DNA was recovered and assessed, by multiplex PCR, for the presence of AGL15 promoter fragments compared to a portion of the coding region of β-2 TUBULIN (At5g62690, Snustad et al., 1992), that is not expected to be bound by AGL15. While the AGL15 promoter fragments and the control fragment were both present in total DNA, the AGL15 promoter fragments containing the potential CArG motifs were enriched after ChIP selection of AGL15 binding sites as shown in Figure 5(a). The two CArGs most proximal to the transcription start site were reproducibly enriched to a greater extent than was CArG1 (Figure 5a and data not shown).

To determine whether AGL15 can bind to its own regulatory regions in tissue where AGL15 is expressed only from the endogenous promoter, we performed ChIP experiments using Brassica napus developing seeds and zygotic embryos. Portions of the 5′ regulatory region of Brassica AGL15-1 and AGL15-2 are available in the database (accession numbers U22665, U22681) and longer sequence information was obtained for AGL15-2 by TAIL-PCR (AY686749). Both Brassica genes contain a conserved region that includes a putative CArG3 site in a similar position and context as found in the 5′ region of Arabidopsis AGL15. A region containing a sequence similar to CArG2 was also identified for AGL15-2. Sequence information did not extend far enough to identify potential CArG1 sites, and no obvious CArG2 site was identified in the sequence available for AGL15-1. DNA fragments containing CArG3 from AGL15-1, and CArG2 and 3 from AGL15-2 were enriched in ChIP populations derived using AGL15 immune serum and B. napus embryos or developing seeds (Figure 5b and data not shown). To assess whether AGL15 binds its own regulatory regions after completion of germination, enrichment assays were performed on Ws and agl15 seedlings. As shown in Figure 5(c), the 5′ regulatory regions were enriched with anti-AGL15 immune serum in ChIP populations derived from Ws seedlings, but not in those from agl15 seedlings. Binding of AGL15 to the individual CArG motifs was further analyzed in vitro by electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA), demonstrating that AGL15, expressed in and purified from Escherichia coli, could bind to CArG2 in vitro, but not to CArG1 or CArG3 (data not shown).

Previous work has demonstrated that the C and G in CArG motifs are highly conserved and important for in vitro binding of MADS-domain proteins (reviewed in Riechmann and Meyerowitz, 1997; Shore and Sharrocks, 1995), including AGL15 (Perry et al., 1996; Tang and Perry, 2003; Wang et al., 2002, 2004). Therefore, these bases were targeted in site-directed mutagenesis of the transgenic construct 1260 to determine the impact of the presence of the three CArG motifs on expression of AGL15 in vivo. Gene expression is regulated by a complex interaction of cis-acting DNA elements and trans-acting regulatory proteins to generate the correct spatial and temporal pattern of gene expression. These interactions may lead to induction or repression of gene expression with a variety of inputs ‘read’ by the basal transcriptional machinery, either via direct interaction or through chromatin remodeling (reviewed in Arnosti, 2003). Deletion analysis is a valuable approach to identify regions containing relevant cis elements.

Deletion analysis of the AGL15 promoter indicated the presence of putative positive and negative cis elements contributing to the expression of AGL15. The region encompassing 1260 bp 5′ of the initiation codon of AGL15 and 524 bp 3′ of the stop codon contained all the elements to drive expression in the same spatial and temporal contexts and to a similar level as a previously reported AGL15:GUS reporter construct (Fernandez et al., 2000). Quantitative analysis of reporter activity was performed in seedlings because this tissue is more accessible than staged developing seeds. However, expression levels of the reporter constructs were qualitatively consistent in all tissues examined (C. Zhu, unpublished data). Deletion of the 3′ region did not change the pattern of expression but reduced the GUS activity observed to less than half that found with inclusion of the 3′ region. This observation is consistent with a role for the 3′ end either in transcriptional control of expression level, or in post-transcriptional controls such as processing, stability of transcript or even translational control, all of which have been reported in the literature (reviewed in Day and Tuite, 1998; Gutiérrez et al., 1999). For the purpose of this study, the 3′ end was included for all other constructs to identify cis regions in the 5′ regulatory regions. The only apparent change in expression pattern in this series was for transgene 1056 where somewhat increased amounts of GUS activity were consistently observed in multiple lines at the root meristem region during embryogenesis (data not shown).

The region from −1260 through −859 was particularly important for expression; any deletions more proximal to the start codon resulted in nearly complete loss of reporter expression. This region included a sequence similar to that corresponding to part of the NDE element from the soybean SAUR15A gene promoter (Xu et al., 1997). The NDE element is generally composed of three regions, none of which is exclusively responsible for auxin response (Xu et al., 1997). The regulatory regions of AGL15 have sequences similar to two of the three elements, and the exact match, CATATG, also appears in the A1 portion of the SAUR 15A promoter that also plays a role in the expression of this gene in response to auxin treatment (Xu et al., 1997). Transgenic plants with reporter transgenes that included this element showed an increase in GUS activity and in GUS transcript levels in response to auxin treatment, whereas loss of this element greatly reduced any response to auxin (Figure 3). Because the specific 956 transgenic line used for these experiments did not have higher GUS activity than the 1260 line used, the lack of increased expression in response to auxin was not due to some maximum level of expression being reached. The endogenous AGL15 gene responded to auxin in both transgenic lines (Figure 3). The small response of transgene 956 to auxin may be mediated by other elements within the promoter or the 3′ region. An auxin response factor binding site was predicted to lie within the 3′ region by searching the database of plant cis-acting regulatory DNA elements (PLACE, http://www.dna.affrc.go.jp/PLACE/wais.html, Higo et al., 1999).

The observation that AGL15 expression is upregulated by auxin was particularly intriguing. Auxin has been long known for its diverse and important roles in many developmental processes (reviewed in Crozier et al., 2000), including somatic embryogenesis where tissue explants are treated with exogenous auxin (e.g., in A. thaliana; Ikeda-Iwai et al., 2002; Luo and Koop, 1997; O'Neill and Mathias, 1993; Pillon et al., 1996; Sangwan et al., 1992; Wu et al., 1992). Auxin is thought to induce embryogenic competence, but the mechanism by which this occurs is unclear (Harada et al., 1998). One Arabidopsis system where exogenous auxin is not required involves culturing zygotic embryos that contain a 35S:AGL15 transgene (Harding et al., 2003). The embryogenic culture established in this system has been stably propagated without exogenous growth regulators for over 7.5 years to date (E. Harding and S.E. Perry, unpublished data). It is intriguing to consider that exogenous auxin may not be needed in this system in part because AGL15 is constitutively expressed from a heterologous promoter. However, auxin is still required in the system of Mordhorst et al. (1998) where embryos are produced from the shoot apical meristem of seedlings that complete germination in liquid media, perhaps reflecting the fact that other factor(s) may be missing in this context. Recently the Medicago truncatula ortholog of SOMATIC EMBRYOGENESIS RECEPTOR KINASE (MtSERK1) was reported to be expressed in response to auxin within 2 days of culture initiation (Nolan et al., 2003), a similar time frame as found for AGL15 (Figure 3 and data not shown). Expression of SERK has been reported as coincident with or able to promote development in an embryonic mode (Hecht et al., 2001; Schmidt et al., 1997; Somleva et al., 2000). Ectopic expression of WUSCHEL in the presence of auxin also promotes embryogenesis, and expression of FUS3, LEC1 and AGL15 is induced by the heat shock treatment that induces WUS expression and further upregulated by auxin, but gene expression was assessed after 2 weeks of treatment by which time embryos were apparent (Gallois et al., 2004).

To identify cis elements important for proper expression from −859 toward the start site, a series of internal deletions were generated where the −1260 to −859 segment was present, but regions from −859 to −128 were deleted. GUS activity increased, in some cases dramatically, or remained similar to transgene 1260 in this series (Figure 1). The transgene that caused the most dramatic increases in GUS activity in the seedlings (Δ859-207, approximately four times the activity observed in 1260 seedlings) also had an obvious and dramatic effect on expression during reproductive development. Notably, GUS activity was persistent in Δ859-207, as well as the more extensive deletion, Δ859-128, in the base of flowers and siliques, even until the silique was nearly mature (Figure 2e,f and data not shown). This was quite different from that reported previously (Fernandez et al., 2000) and for transgene 1260 (Figure 2d) where GUS activity could be detected in young flower buds and at the base of older buds, but was absent near the time of anthesis. These results may indicate elements within the −302 to −206 region that repress AGL15 expression during late flower development. To test whether continuous expression of AGL15 in the base of floral organs from its own regulatory regions was sufficient to delay senescence and/or abscission of perianth organs, AGL15 was expressed from a modified form of its own regulatory regions with deletion of −859 through −207. However, no changes in perianth retention were apparent (data not shown). This agrees with Fang and Fernandez (2002), where even overexpression of AGL15 within the abscission zone was insufficient to delay abscission of perianth organs.

GUS activity of transgene 1260 in seedlings, as well as other tissues such as flower buds, is dramatically decreased when in the AGL15 overexpressor (35S:AGL15) background (Figure 4a,b and data not shown). Conversely, the 1260 transgene yields significantly more GUS activity when present in a loss-of-function agl15 background (Figure 4c) indicating that the potential negative autoregulation is not simply an artifact of the overexpression construct. Finally, a modified form of AGL15 that contains a strong transcriptional activation domain (the VP16-domain, Sadowski et al., 1988) was able to cause a dramatic increase and expansion of 1260-driven GUS activity within seedlings compared with wild-type siblings from a genetic cross (Figure 4d). All the above support a role for AGL15 in autoregulation to restrict levels of expression.

Autoregulation of AGL15 expression could be the result of direct or indirect regulation, or both. At least three potential binding sites for MADS-domain proteins are present within the 5′ regulatory regions of AGL15. Two of these sites, CArG1 and 2, are of a divergent C-8-G form with a longer than standard A/T-rich domain than the canonical CC-6-GG form. The C-8-G form is preferentially recognized by AGL15 in vitro (Tang and Perry, 2003). Enrichment tests on populations of chromatin that have been selected for direct association of AGL15 by ChIP indicate that at least part of the autoregulatory effects may be due to direct regulation (Figure 5). 5′ regulatory regions were found to be over-represented compared with total DNA or control immunoprecipitations using pre-immune sera, not only in embryogenic culture tissue constitutively expressing AGL15 via a 35S:AGL15 transgene, but also in Brassica zygotic embryos and in Arabidopsis wild-type seedlings where AGL15 is expressed only from its endogenous regulatory sequences. Notably, seedlings that are unable to accumulate AGL15 due to an insertional mutation show no enrichment of AGL15 5′ regulatory regions (Figure 5c). EMSA experiments confirmed that AGL15 can bind in a sequence-specific manner to CArG2 in vitro (data not shown). Although AGL15 was not found to bind to CArG1 or CArG3 in EMSA, it should be remembered that EMSAs represent an in vitro context independent of other co-factors or chromatin architecture, and lack of binding in EMSA in no way negates the evidence from enrichment tests that AGL15 binds to DNA fragments including CArG1 and 3 in vivo. Other MADS-domain proteins have also been reported to bind sites in vivo that were not recognized in vitro (Black et al., 1996; Molkentin et al., 1995).

The site-directed mutagenesis experiments are supportive of direct autoregulation of AGL15 by its own gene product. Mutation of CArG1 produced a slight, but not significant reduction of GUS activity levels (Figure 6). This was also the CArG motif-containing DNA fragment that was reproducibly least enriched in ChIP populations using AGL15-specific serum (Figure 5a and data not shown). The decrease is consistent with the 5′ deletion experiments where loss of approximately 100 bp from the 5′ end and including CArG1 decreased GUS activity. Mutation of CArG2 nearly doubled GUS activity compared with 1260, indicating a role for this site in negative regulation of expression. This finding is consistent with the internal deletion construct Δ859-639, that has deleted CArG2, and possibly other important cis elements, resulting in a more than twofold increase in GUS activity compared with 1260. The next dramatic increase in GUS activity was observed when CArG3 was deleted in the Δ859-207 transgene where activity has nearly doubled again compared with Δ859-639. However, site-directed mutagenesis of CArG3 led to a decrease in GUS activity in seedlings, indicating that this site may be involved in upregulation of AGL15. Therefore, other important elements appear to have been lost in the Δ859-207 construct. This could include additional site(s) important for autoregulation because when a construct with site-directed mutations in CArG2 and 3 was introduced into agl15 plants, the level of GUS activity was nearly twofold that found for this construct in the wild-type background (data not shown). Also supportive of additional sites for autoregulation is the fact that constitutive expression by the 35S:AGL15 transgene led to reduction of GUS activity for 1260m2m3 plants (data not shown). No other canonical MADS-domain binding sites are present, but AGL15 may regulate its expression through a non-canonical site, or directly through protein–protein interactions with other trans-acting factors bound to diverse cis elements (Morin et al., 2000) or even through indirect pathways with intermediate transcriptional regulators.

Repressive and stimulatory effects of a transcriptional regulator at different binding sites has been previously reported (reviewed in Latchman, 2001) and it is possible that AGL15 first stimulates expression through CArG3, and as increased amounts of AGL15 accumulate, binding to other cis elements such as CArG2 lead to repression of expression. Other MADS-box genes are involved in autoregulatory loops. In animals, the MADS-domain protein MEF2 participates in a positive direct autoregulatory loop to maintain myogenic programs (Cripps et al., 2004). In Antirrhinum flowers, DEFICIENS and GLOBOSA (Schwarz-Sommer et al., 1992; Tröbner et al., 1992), and their counterparts in Arabidopsis, APETALA3 and PISTILLATA (Hill et al., 1998; Tilly et al., 1998), form heterodimers to contribute to their own expression. In some cases, multiple CArG motifs involved in expression have been identified within the 5′ regulatory regions (Hill et al., 1998; Schwarz-Sommer et al., 1992; Tilly et al., 1998), but in other cases there are no obvious MADS-domain protein binding sites within the region responsible for autoregulation (Chen et al., 2000). This may indicate a role for indirect regulation, or the necessity for co-factors to facilitate binding perhaps to a non-canonical type CArG motif or via protein–protein interactions. As found for AGL15, multiple CArG motifs are present within the APETALA3 promoter that appear to act in a redundant manner within the context of the native promoter, and that individually contribute to expression or repression of APETALA3 (Tilly et al., 1998).

In summary, we have identified a region of the AGL15 promoter involved in auxin response, as well as regions important for cessation of expression at the bases of flower organs nearing anthesis. We have also presented evidence that AGL15 regulates its own expression and identified at least two cis elements involved in this response. Indeed, AGL15 expression fulfills the major tests for direct regulation: direct association with regulatory regions (Figure 5); and response of regulatory regions to AGL15 amounts (Figure 4).

Two AGL15 genes are present in B. napus, AGL15-1 and AGL15-2 (Heck et al., 1995). However, the AGL15-2 sequence available in the database was not long enough to include a potential CArG2 box. To further extend the 5′ flanking sequence, thermal asymmetrical interlaced TAIL-PCR (Liu et al., 1995) was employed. PCR reactions were conducted on MJ research PTC 100, using TaKaRa Ex TaqTM superscript polymerase (Panvera, Madison, WI, USA). Two rounds of PCR amplifications were used to isolate DNA upstream of the known portion of the AGL15-2 promoter. Approximately 15 pmol of the AGL15-2-specific primer was used with 150 pmol of the partially degenerate primer AD-2 (5′-NGTCGASWGANAWGAA-3′) for the first PCR reaction. PCR was performed as follows: (i) 96°C for 5 min; (ii) 94°C for 10 sec; (iii) 65°C for 30 sec; (iv) 72°C for 1 min; (v) repeat four additional cycles of steps 2 through 4; (vi) 94°C for 10 sec; (vii) 25°C for 3 min; (viii) ramp to 72°C over 3 min; (ix) 72°C for 3 min; (x) 94°C for 10 sec; (xi) 65°C for 30 sec; (xii) 72°C for 1 min; (xiii) repeat one more cycle of steps 10 through 13; (xiv) 94°C for 10 sec; (xv) 44°C for 1 min; (xvi) 72°C for 1 min; (xvii) repeat 14 more cycles of steps 10 through 16; (xviii) 72°C for 3 min; and (xix) 4°C.

The product from the first PCR was diluted 1:50, and 1 μl of the dilution was used for the second round of PCR. In the second PCR, 15 pmol of a second nested AGL15-specific primer and 15 pmol of AD-2 were used. The PCR conditions were: (i) 96°C for 5 min; (ii) 94°C for 10 sec; (iii) 61°C for 30 sec; (iv) 72°C for 1 min; (v) repeat one more cycle of steps 2 through 4; (vi) 94°C for 10 sec; (vii) 44°C for 1 min; (viii) 72°C for 1 min; (ix) repeat 17 additional cycles of steps 2 through 8; (x) 72°C for 4 min; and (xi) 4°C.

A second series of TAIL-PCR reactions was performed to further extend the sequence information. The products were cloned into pGEM®-T vector and sequenced.
