Abstract

The key transcription factors that control seed maturation, ABSCISIC ACID INSENSITIVE3 (ABI3) and FUSCA3 (FUS3), share homologous DNA-binding domains. Regulation of seed storage protein genes At2S3 and CRC by ABI3 and FUS3 was investigated using transgenic plants in which ABI3 and FUS3 could be ectopically induced by steroid hormones. Like ABI3, the presence of FUS3 led to expression of At2S3 and CRC in vegetative tissues. FUS3-mediated induction of CRC was completely dependent on exogenous abscisic acid (ABA), while At2S3 was weakly induced without ABA but strongly enhanced with ABA. This ABA dependency of FUS3-induced CRC and At2S3 expression was similar to that observed for ABI3. However, kinetic analysis revealed distinctions between the mechanisms of ABA-dependent CRC regulation by FUS3 or ABI3, and between target genes. While At2S3 activation by FUS3 was rapid, CRC induction by FUS3 in the presence of ABA, and by ABA followed by the presence of FUS3, took a significantly longer time (24–36 h). This suggested the involvement of an indirect mechanism requiring the ABA- and FUS3-dependent synthesis of intermediate regulatory factor(s). A chimeric protein composed of the FUS3 B3 domain, and a heterologous activation domain and nuclear localization signal exhibited a tight coupling with ABA regulation as observed for wild-type FUS3. Simultaneous induction of FUS3 and ABI3 did not result in the synergistic activation of CRC and At2S3. Based on these results, similarities and differences in the mechanisms of seed storage protein gene regulation by FUS3 and ABI3 are discussed.

Introduction

Seed development in angiosperms can be divided roughly into two major phases (West and Harada 1993). The first phase is embryo development in which cell division and growth start from the fertilized egg and continue until morphogenesis is complete. Growth is halted and the embryo then enters the maturation phase. During the maturation phase, the embryo acquires the various characteristics of a seed, including accumulation of nutrient reserves, desiccation tolerance and dormancy. Seed formation allows plants to avoid developing under adverse conditions, such as water deficiency and extreme temperatures, such that the seeds can germinate and restart growth when favorable conditions are available.

Genetic and physiological studies have demonstrated that the plant hormone abscisic acid (ABA) plays a critical role in the regulation of seed maturation (McCarty 1995). In addition, several genetic loci that control the maturation program have been identified, including ABA INSENSITIVE3 (ABI3), FUSCA3 (FUS3) and LEAFY COTYLEDON1 (LEC1), that encode transcription factors (Robichaud et al. 1980, Koornneef et al. 1984, Giraudat et al. 1992, Meinke 1992, Bäumlein et al. 1994, Keith et al. 1994, Meinke et al. 1994, West et al. 1994, Lotan et al. 1998, Luerßen et al. 1998, Stone et al. 2001). Both ABI3 and FUS3 contain B3 DNA-binding domains that show high sequence similarity to each other (Giraudat et al. 1992, Luerßen et al. 1998). In addition to direct binding to DNA via the B3 domain, ABI3 also functions as a co-activator by interacting with ABA-response element (ABRE)-binding factors (Carson et al. 1997, Hattori et al. 1992, Hobo et al. 1999, Nakamura et al. 2001). LEC1 encodes a homolog of the CCAAT-binding factor HAP3 subunit (Lotan et al. 1998). Plants that carry mutations in these genes have defects in the accumulation of storage reserves such as lipids and storage proteins, dormancy, inhibition of precocious germination and desiccation tolerance (Koornneef et al. 1984, Finkelstein and Somerville 1990, Meinke 1992, Nambara et al. 1992, Bäumlein et al. 1994, Keith et al. 1994, Meinke et al. 1994, Parcy et al. 1994, West et al. 1994, Nambara et al. 1995, Parcy et al. 1997, Raz et al. 2001). As these genes regulate a broad range of maturation events, they are considered as master regulators of the maturation program. LEAFY COTYLEDON2 (LEC2) also encodes a transcription factor with a B3 domain similar to that of ABI3 and FUS3 (Stone et al. 2001). While lec2 mutants display morphologically similar phenotypes to those of fus3 and lec1 (Meinke et al. 1994), monogenic lec2 mutations do not affect maturation to the same extent as mutation of lec1 and fus3, with desiccation tolerance and storage material accumulation less affected (Meinke et al. 1994). However, a recent study has shown that LEC2 and FUS3 bind to the same cis-acting elements and function in a partially redundant manner in terms of seed storage protein gene regulation (Kroj et al. 2003).

Although lec1, fus3 and abi3 mutants exhibit common maturation-related phenotypes, lec1 and fus3 mutants differ morphologically from abi3 mutants in that they exhibit leafy traits in cotyledons, as represented by trichome formation and anthocyanin accumulation (Meinke 1992, Bäumlein et al. 1994, Keith et al. 1994, West et al. 1994). abi3 mutant seeds also display unique phenotypes, such as failure in chlorophyll degradation and ABA insensitivity upon germination, which distinguish abi3 mutants from fus3 and lec1, the leafy cotyledon (lec)-type mutants (Koornneef et al. 1984, Finkelstein and Somerville 1990, Meinke 1992, Nambara et al. 1992, Parcy et al. 1994, Nambara et al. 1995, Parcy et al. 1997). The similarity between the fus3 and lec1 mutant phenotypes suggests that wild-type fus3 and lec1 regulate a common set of downstream genes. Despite the phenotypes that distinguish the lec-type and abi3 mutants, the two types of loci genetically interact as the phenotypes specific to the monogenic mutant of either type are enhanced in digenic mutants (Parcy et al. 1997, Nambara et al. 2000, Raz et al. 2001). Synergism is also observed for phenotypes common to lec-type and abi3 mutations (Parcy et al. 1997, Nambara et al. 2000). Expression of storage protein genes, such as At2S and cruciferins, which are suppressed but not completely lost in monogenic lec-type or abi3, is not observed in lec-type and abi3 digenic mutants (Parcy et al. 1997). Similar genetic interactions are also observed between these loci and ABA-deficient loci (Parcy et al. 1997, Nambara et al. 2000).

The LEC-type genes also function in developmental aspects differently from ABI3. Both types of genes are required for developmental arrest during seed development, which has been shown to be secured by at least two sequential processes, embryo growth arrest and embryo dormancy. The LEC-type genes and ABI3 have been implicated in the former and the latter process, respectively (Raz et al. 2001). Despite their role as master regulators of complex developmental processes, in which intricate regulatory cascades are likely to be involved, several lines of evidence have suggested that ABI3 and/or FUS3 are directly involved in transcriptional regulation of seed storage protein genes and LEA genes, which are considered to represent terminal outputs of the maturation program (Hattori et al. 1992, Hattori et al. 1995, Vasil et al. 1995, Carson et al. 1997, Suzuki et al. 1997, Luerßen et al. 1998, Hobo et al. 1999, Ezcurra et al. 2000, Nakamura et al. 2001).

In this report, we investigated the effect of ectopic induction of ABI3 and FUS3 on the expression of seed storage protein and LEA genes in the vegetative tissues of transgenic plants to gain insight into the molecular mechanisms of action of these transcription factors and to illuminate the differences and similarities in their modes of action. By following the time course of induction of target genes using the glucocorticoid or estrogen (Estr) induction system (Aoyama and Chua 1997, Zuo et al. 2000), we demonstrated that ABI3 and FUS3 regulate storage protein genes in a complex manner that could not have been revealed by loss-of-function experiments or conventional gain-of-function type analyses using transgenic plants that constitutively express these factors.

Results

Ectopic expression in vegetative tissues of ABI3 or FUS3 induces expression of seed storage protein genes

We generated transgenic Arabidopsis plants, in which FUS3, ABI3 or their derivatives could be induced by glucocorticoid (dexamethasone; Dex) treatment by using pTA7001 T-DNA binary vector developed by Aoyama and Chua (1997). Transgenic plants, TA-FUS3 and TA-FUS3-DH (the prefix TA- stands for the pTA7001 vector used for transformation) carried the FUS3 coding sequence and that with a double hemagglutinin epitope/polyhistidine affinity tag (dHA-His; Kagaya et al. 2002), respectively, under the control of a chimeric promoter regulated by a GAL4–VP16–glucocorticoid receptor fusion protein (GVG). Similarly, TA-ABI3 and TA-ABI3-DH plants carried the FUS3 coding sequence and that with the dHA-His tag, respectively, as a Dex-inducible transgene. TA plants were control transgenic plants transformed with the empty pTA7001 vector.

Treatment of young TA-FUS transgenic Arabidopsis plants (7 d old) with Dex resulted in the accumulation of FUS3 mRNA, which reached maximal levels within 6 h (Fig. 1A). Immunoblot analysis of TA-FUS-DH plants with anti-HA antibody showed that FUS3 protein accumulation was observed as early as 3 h after Dex treatment and reached maximal levels by 6 h (Fig. 1B). We also confirmed similar induction of the ABI3 transcripts and dHA-His-tagged ABI3 protein in TA-ABI3 and TA-ABI3-DH transgenic plants, respectively (data not shown).

Previous studies have shown that ectopic expression of ABI3 driven by the cauliflower mosaic virus (CaMV) 35S promoter in vegetative tissues resulted in ectopic expression of the At2S and CRC seed storage protein genes in an ABA-dependent manner (Parcy et al. 1994). ABA-dependent ectopic expression of these genes was confirmed in TA-ABI3 transgenic plants treated with Dex in the presence or absence of ABA (Fig. 2). Low levels of ABI3-induced ectopic At2S3 expression in the absence of ABA were observed in Dex-treated TA-ABI3 plants. In contrast, CRC induction by ABI3 in these plants was completely ABA dependent (Fig. 2). In Dex-treated TA-FUS3 plants, both ectopic CRC expression and ABA-independent At2S3 induction was observed, similar to TA-ABI3 plants. However, ABA-independent At2S3 expression levels relative to ABA-induced levels were higher in plants expressing FUS3 than in those expressing ABI3. The induction of At2S3 by FUS3 in the absence of ABA was transient; the transcript levels declined after 24 h and reached a very low level at 48 h. This decline in the At2S3 transcript level after 12 h appeared partly due to the decline in the FUS3 level, but may be partly due to some unknown down-regulation mechanism or ABA-independent FUS3 activation of At2S3.

In contrast to At2S3, induction of CRC by FUS3 was totally ABA dependent, as found for ABI3. Ectopic induction of these seed storage protein genes by FUS3 or ABI3 was also observed in older plants (e.g. 14-day-old plants; data not shown). These results indicated that FUS3 acted like ABI3 to activate seed-specific genes in vegetative tissues when expressed ectopically. Our results also showed a difference between At2S3 and CRC expression patterns in response to FUS3 or ABI3.

LEA genes, such as AtEm1 and AtEm6, are expressed abundantly during late embryogenesis. Although their expression is not truly seed specific (Vicient et al. 2000), the induction of AtEm1 and AtEm6 by ABA in vegetative tissues is strongly enhanced by ectopic expression of ABI3 (Parcy et al. 1994). In agreement with previous studies, Dex-treated TA-ABI3 plants showed greatly enhanced ABA-mediated induction of LEA genes AtEm1 and AtEm6 (Fig. 2). However, this enhancement was not observed in TA-FUS3 plants. While some increase in LEA gene expression levels was observed after Dex treatment in TA-FUS plants, this was considered to be a side effect of GVG expression as similar results were observed in TA transgenic plants transformed with empty TA7001 vector (Fig. 2).

Induction kinetics suggest distinct mechanisms of FUS3 and ABI3 action

While CRC induction in TA-ABI3 plants became obvious 6 h after simultaneous Dex and ABA treatment (Fig. 2, see also Fig. 4), CRC induction required 24 h treatment in TA-FUS3 plants. For At2S3, expression was first observed 6 h after simultaneous Dex and ABA treatment in both TA-ABI3 and TA-FUS3 plants. The induction kinetics of At2S3 appeared to be rapid, given the kinetics of FUS3 protein accumulation shown in Fig. 1. However, up-regulation of AtEm1 and AtEm6 following Dex-induced ABI3 was even more rapid; the enhancing effects were already near maximum at 3 h. The ABA-independent induction of At2S3 by Dex in TA-ABI3 plants was 6 h slower compared with induction due to simultaneous Dex and ABA treatment (Fig. 2, see also Fig. 4). These results indicated that the mechanisms of FUS3 and ABI3 regulation were distinct, depending on the downstream target gene. In particular, the slow kinetics of ABA-dependent CRC induction by FUS3 suggested that FUS3 regulation of CRC involved a complex process likely to include the FUS3 and/or ABA-dependent gene expression leading to synthesis or repression of intermediate regulatory factor(s).

To clarify these points, transgenic plants were pre-treated with ABA for 36 h and then treated with Dex, or pre-treated with Dex for 36 h and then treated with ABA, and the time course of CRC, At2S3, AtEm1 and AtEm6 induction was followed (Fig. 3). If the induction of the putative intermediate factor(s) required either ABA or FUS3 alone, either FUS3 or ABA pre-treatment would reslut in the immediate induction by the other. However, CRC induction by ABA in Dex-pre-treated TA-FUS3 plants was observed after 36 h (Fig. 3, 4), and the induction by Dex in ABA-pre-treated transgenic plants took place at between 24 and 36 h (Fig. 3, 4). These results supported the hypothesis that CRC induction is dependent on intermediate regulatory factor(s), the expression of which is regulated by both ABA and FUS3. Another possiblity is that both factors regulated independently by ABA and FUS3 are involved. It should be noted that simultaneous treatment with ABA and Dex appeared to accelerate CRC induction. However, this difference could have resulted from the prolonged incubation period with either ABA or Dex.

Although Dex-induced FUS3 expression gradually declined after 6 h until 36 h of treatment (Fig. 1), the FUS3 trascript levels remained constant thereafter during ABA treatment of Dex-pre-treated TA-FUS3 plants for 36 h (Fig. 3). However, the level of FUS3 expression was increased at 48 h of ABA treatment. Therefore, the higher level of CRC expression at 48 h compared with the level at 36 h in Dex-pre-treated TA-FUS3 plants could be due partly to the increased level of FUS3. Nevertheless, this does not affect the conclusion that both ABA and FUS3 are required for the induction of the putative intermediate factor(s). ABA did not significantly affect Dex-induced FUS3 transcript levels when treated simultaneously, although there was a tendency to suppress slightly the decline observed after 6 h (data not shown).

Increased At2S3 expression in Dex-pre-treated plants was apparent at 12 h after ABA treatment, while Dex treatment of ABA-pre-treated plants resulted in At2S3 induction within 6 h (Fig. 3, 4). With a high contrast imgage, weak At2S3 signals were detectable at 0, 3 and 6 h in these plants. The low level of ABA-independent At2S3 expression at 0 h in Dex-pre-treated plants is due to the decreased expression at 48 h in the plants treated with Dex alone as observed in Fig. 2.

Similar pre-treatment experiments were performed with TA-ABI3 transgenic plants (Fig. 3). In Dex-pre-treated TA-ABI3 plants, induction of At2S3 and CRC was detected at 3 and 12 h, respectively, after ABA treatment (Fig. 3, 4). In ABA-pre-treated plants, the induction of At2S3 and CRC was detected at 3 and 6 h, respectively, after Dex treatment (Fig. 3, 4). The high level of AtEm1 and AtEm6 induction in Dex-pre-treated TA-ABI3 plants in response to ABA occurred by 3 h, similar to the low level of induction observed in the absence of ABI3 (Fig. 3, 4). The enhancing effect of ABI3 on AtEm1 and AtEm6 induction in ABA-pre-treated plants was also observed within 3 h (Fig. 3, 4). Thus, the up-regulation of At2S3 and CRC by ABI3 and ABA was more rapid than the induction of CRC by FUS3 and ABA, but not as rapid as up-regulation of AtEm1 and AtEm6.

Ectopic expression of FUS3 does not induce expression of ABI3, ABI4 and LEC1

Previous studies have revealed cross-regulation of ABI3, ABI4 and ABI5 at the level of transcript accumulation. These genes have been genetically characterized and shown to encode transcription factors involved in ABA-mediated regulation in seeds (Giraudat et al. 1992, Finkelstein et al. 1998, Finkelstein and Lynch 2000, Lopez-Molina and Chua 2000, Söderman et al. 2000, Brocard et al. 2002). In addition to ABI3, ectopic expression of ABI4 and LEC1 has also been shown to result in the expression of seed storage protein genes in vegetative tissues (Lotan et al. 1998, Söderman et al. 2000). As time course analysis of CRC induction by ectopic FUS3 and ABA suggested the involvement of intermediate regulatory factors, we tested whether ABI3, ABI4 or LEC1 were induced by FUS3. However, none of these genes was induced by FUS3, with or without ABA (Fig. 5).

The FUS3 C-terminal region is essential for activation function and can be substituted with a heterologous activation domain

Despite the above-described differences in the mechanisms by which FUS3 and ABI3 regulate seed storage protein genes, they share similarities in their molecular structures (Luerßen et al. 1998). In the middle of the FUS3 molecule lies a B3 DNA-binding domain that is highly homologous to the B3 domain of ABI3. The N-terminal region contains a basic sequence that shows weak similarity to the B2 region of ABI3 (Fig. 6A) and may act as a nuclear localization signal (NLS) (Luerßen et al. 1998). In contrast, the C-terminal region of FUS exhibits no similarity to ABI3 or to any other known protein. To gain insight into the molecular mechanisms of FUS3 action and to compare those of ABI3, the function of each domain was analyzed (Fig. 6).

Six FUS3 derivatives were constructed and transformed into Arabidopsis using the pER8 vector that allowed Estr-inducible expression of the transgene (Zuo et al. 2000) (Fig. 6A). Three constructs, dC1, dC2 and dC3, contained serial FUS3 C-terminal deletions. In NLS-dN1, the N-terminal region that contained the B2-like domain was deleted and replaced with an NLS from SV40, to compensate for the putative NLS function of the B2-like sequence. In NLS-B3, the same N-terminal deletion and NLS insertion as in NLS-dN was made in dC2. In NLS-AD-B3, the VP16 activation domain was inserted between the NLS and B3 domain of NLS-B3.

ER-FUS3 transgenic plants that contained Estr-inducible wild-type FUS3 behaved essentially in the same manner as TA-FUS3 plants with respect to CRC and At2S3 induction. In the presence of Estr and ABA, both seed storage protein genes were strongly induced (Fig. 6B). However, all the C-terminal deletions (dC1, dC2 and dC3) failed to induce CRC and At2S3. These results indicated that the 53 C-terminal amino acids were essential for the activation of downstream genes. As NSL-dN induced CRC and At2S3, no specific function was defined for the N-terminal region that contained the B2-like structure, except for its putative role as an NLS. Although lower levels of CRC and At2S3 induction were observed in NLS-dN plants compared with wild-type ER-FUS3 plants, this was thought to be due to the lower level of transgene expression rather than to a partial loss of function. NLS-B3, in which the entire C-terminal region from NLS-dN1 had been deleted, lost the ability to induce CRC and At2S3, but the activity was recovered by the addition of the VP16 activation domain (AD-NLS-B3; Fig. 6B). This result was consistent with the recent report that the C-terminal domain of FUS3 functioned as a transcriptional activation domain (Monke et al. 2004). Notably, activation of CRC and At2S3 by NLS-AD-B3, in which only the B3 domain originated from FUS3, showed essentially the same ABA dependencies as wild-type FUS3.

Simultaneous induction of FUS3 and ABI3 does not result in the synergistic activation of CRC and At2S3, but gives only additive effects

In the developing seed, loss-of-function mutations in either FUS3 or ABI3 result in greatly reduced levels of seed storage protein gene transcripts, including those of CRC and At2S3. This suggests that FUS3 and ABI3 synergistically activate the transcription of seed storage protein genes. To test for a synergistic interaction between FUS3 and ABI3 in the ectopic activation of CRC and At2S3, we obtained a TA-ABI3/ER-FUS3 double transgenic plant in which FUS3 and ABI3 could be induced by Dex and Estr, respectively, separately or simultaneously. Induction of FUS3 alone by Estr or ABI3 alone by Dex in the presence of ABA resulted in the activation of CRC and At2S3 with similar kinetics to those observed in previous experiments (Fig. 2), except that down-regulation of At2S3 expression was observed 12 h after Estr treatment. Simultaneous induction of FU3 and ABI3 led to only slightly higher CRC expression levels and similar At2S3 levels compared with the levels obtained with ABI3 alone. Synergistic activation, as expected from previous genetic analyses, was not observed, such that the effects of both ABI3 and FUS3 appeared to be only additive at best.

Discussion

In this study, we showed that ectopic induction of FUS3, like ABI3, led to the ectopic expression of both types of seed storage protein genes, At2S3 and CRC, in ABA-dependent or -independent manners. Although the effects of ectopic ABI3 expression have been reported previously, the use of Dex- and Estr-inducible gene expression systems allowed us to gain new insights into the mechanisms of ABI3 and FUS3 actions that could not be revealed by conventional constitutive promoter-based over-expression assays or analyses using loss-of-function mutants.

At least two distinct mechanisms of action are known for the ABI3 transcription factor. First, ABI3 functions as a co-activator that interacts with ABRE-binding factors (Hattori et al. 1992, Carson et al. 1997, Hobo et al. 1999, Nakamura et al. 2001). Secondly, it directly binds to RY/Sph elements via its B3 domain (Suzuki et al. 1997, Monke et al. 2004). ABI3 is thought to act on seed storage protein gene promoters based on the following observations: (i) most seed storage protein genes, including At2S3 and CRC, contain RY/Sph elements and ABREs (Dickinson et al. 1998); (ii) these sequences have been shown to act as functional cis-elements (Bäumlein et al. 1992, Chamberland et al. 1992, Lelievre et al. 1992, Fujiwara and Beachy 1994, Ellerström et al. 1996); and (iii) transactivation of seed storage protein gene promoters by ABI3 has been shown to be dependent on the presence of RY/Sph elements (Bobb et al. 1997, Ezcurra et al. 2000). Similarly to ABI3, FUS3 has also been demonstrated to bind RY/Sph elements in the promoters of At2S3 and Brassica napA, which encodes 2S albumin, and transactivates them through the RY/Sph elements (Reidt et al. 2000, Kroj et al. 2003, Monke et al. 2004). This indicates that FUS3 can act directly on seed storage protein genes.

Despite the evidence for direct action of ABI3 and FUS3 on seed storage protein genes, our kinetic analysis of CRC expression in Dex-treated TA-FUS transgenic plants indicated that CRC activation by FUS3 involved a more complex process. CRC induction in TA-FUS plants required 24–36 h to occur following Dex treatment, whereas it took only 6–12 h in TA-ABI3 plants. We obtained essentially the same results with ER-FUS3 plants, in which FUS3 could be induced by Estr (Fig. 7; A. Yamamoto, unpublished results). Although Northern analysis detected the CRC transcript at 3–12 h after Estr and ABA treatment, reverse transcription–polymerase chain reaction (RT–PCR) analysis with these transgenic plants detected the products derived from the CRC transcript after extended cycles of PCR (40 cycles) at 6 h and thereafter. However, quantitative RT–PCR analysis (real-time PCR) indicated that the CRC transcript levels at 3, 6 or 12 h of Estr and ABA treatment were <0.2% of the 48 h level, which was below the limit of quantification with the used primer set and indistinguishable from the value obtained with untransformed Col plants (A. Yamamoto, unpublished results). Thus, FUS3 could also act on the CRC promoter directly to trigger transcription in the presence of ABA at a very low efficiency, although the major control of CRC by FUS3 and ABA depends on the induction of putative secondary regulatory factor(s).

However, this was found not to be the case as induction of ABI3 in TA-FUS3 plants was not observed. The ABA induction of CRC in the presence of FUS3 also took 24 h, a significantly longer time period than that required for ABA induction of At2S3 in the presence of FUS3 or ABI3. These results suggested that FUS3/ABA-dependent synthesis of intermediate regulatory factor(s) was involved in FUS3/ABA-mediated induction of CRC. Another possibility is that CRC induction might require FUS3/ABA-dependent repression or inactivation of a negative regulator. It has been reported recently that expression of the TTG1 gene, a regulator of both anthocyanin production and trichome formation in post-embryonic development, is negatively regulated by FUS3, and that most fus3 phenotypes, including defects in seed storage protein accumulation, were recovered by ttg1 mutation (Tsuchiya et al. 2004). However, this suppressive effect of ttg1 was observed for both cruciferin and 2S albumin expression (Tsuchiya et al. 2004). In addition, expression of TTG1 in ER-FUS3 transgenic plants was not suppressed by FUS3 induction either, with or without ABA (A. Yamamoto et al., unpublished results). Thus, it is unlikely that the process involved in the relatively slow induction of CRC by FUS3 and ABA involves the TTG1 pathway.

The construct that consisted of the FUS3 B3-DNA binding domain, a VP16 transcriptional activation domain and an SV40 NLS functioned like wild-type FUS3. The finding that the C-terminal activation domain could be replaced by a non-specific heterologous transcriptional activation domain indicated that FUS3 was likely to function as an activator of a positive regulatory factor(s) that are intermediates in CRC induction rather than as a repressor of a negative factor(s). Unfortunately, protein synthesis inhibitors could not be used to confirm whether newly synthesized protein was required because the induction of FUS3 by Dex involved protein synthesis.

The identity of the FUS3/ABA-regulated intermediate factor required for CRC induction remains unknown. It could be a signal transduction factor, such as a protein kinase, phosphatase or a cascade of such factors, not present in vegetative tissues. ABA-dependent activation of seed storage protein genes by ABI3 in vegetative tissues appears to require an ABA signaling system that was not affected by abi1 mutation (Parcy and Giraudat 1997). Since abi1 mutation inhibits most aspects of the ABA responses in vegetative tissues, a seed-specific ABA signaling system may also exist, the expression of which is regulated by ABI3 and/or FUS3. However, in the simplest model, the putative intermediate regulatory factor would be a transcription factor, and some FUS3-regulated seed-specific transcription factors have already been described (Kirik et al. 1998a, Kirik et al. 1998b, Brocard et al. 2002).

Cross-regulation between transcription factors involved in seed maturation has been described, and both hierarchical and combinatorial controls have been suggested in the regulation of seed maturation (Parcy et al. 1997, Söderman et al. 2000, Brocard et al. 2002, Brocard-Gifford et al. 2003; Kroj et al. 2003). However, the regulation of seed storage protein gene transcription by FUS3 has been studied previously only with respect to its direct action on target gene promoters. In this regard, we have presented the first evidence for an indirect mechanism of FUS3 action on seed storage protein genes, critical to the activation of the CRC gene.

Severe mutations in both ABI3 and FUS3 greatly reduce the accumulation of seed storage protein gene transcripts in developing seeds. Such genetic analyses suggest a highly synergistic or interdependent action between FUS3 and ABI3 in the regulation of the seed storage protein genes. However, such an interaction was not observed when tested by ectopic expression in vegetative tissues. The failure to detect any interaction between FUS3 and ABI3 in our ectopic system could be due to a lack of factors that aid the interaction, which were perhaps not induced by ABI3 and FUS3 in vegetative tissues. Alternatively, FUS3 or ABI3 in the developing seed may regulate factors required for the other to function, or define the developmental state in which the other functions. The apparent requirement for both ABI3 and FUS3 in the activation of seed storage protein genes in developing seeds might not be a reflection of a mechanistic interaction between the two factors but of complex genetic interactions.

Our analyses revealed that FUS3 induction of CRC was strictly dependent on ABA signaling. Such tight coupling between ABA signaling and FUS3 in seed storage protein gene regulation has not been reported previously. In contrast, ABI3 is known to function, at least in part, as a mediator of ABA signals. One known mechanism of interaction between ABI3 and ABA signaling is the physical interaction of an ABRE-binding factor with a region of ABI3 outside the B3 domain (Hobo et al. 1999, Nakamura et al. 2001). The mechanism for coupling between FUS3 and ABA signaling appears to be different as FUS3 regulated CRC expression in a completely ABA-dependent manner even after sequences outside the B3 DNA-binding domain were replaced by heterologous non-specific functional domains. The FUS3–ABA signaling interaction could occur either at the CRC promoter or at the promoter of the gene for the putative intermediate regulatory factor.

The enhancing effect of ABI3 on the ABA induction of AtEm1 and AtEm6 was already near maximum levels at 3 h. ABA-mediated induction of these genes was similarly rapid. Therefore, it is likely that ABI3 acted directly on the LEA gene promoters. The time required for initiation of At2S3 induction by either FUS3 or ABI3 (6–12 h) was relatively long compared with the time required for ABI3 to enhance the induction of the LEA genes although it was still fast, given the time required for the FUS3 protein to accumulate to a maximum level. Therefore, induction of At2S3 by FUS3 and CRC by ABI3 or FUS3 also may require intermediary processes. This does not necessarily exclude the direct action of ABI3 and FUS3, but rather suggests that both direct and indirect actions may be involved, considering the interaction between the RY/Sph element and ABI3 or FUS3 (Suzuki et al. 1997, Reidt et al. 2000). Alternatively, the longer induction time may reflect differences in mechanisms of action. While the effect of ABI3 on AtEm1 and AtEm6 was found not to be B3 dependent, B3 was absolutely required for induction of CRC and At2S3 (unpublished results).

The initiation of At2S3 induction by ABA in the presence of ABI3 was obvious after 3 h of ABA treatment, and was as rapid as the induction of AtEm1 and AtEm6. The results suggest that the primary ABA signaling mechanism that does not require protein synthesis is sufficient for the initiation of At2S3 induction. However, a secondary ABA signaling may also be operating because the level of At2S3 expression increased further after 24 h of ABA treatment in Dex-pre-treated TA-ABI3. At2S3 induction by ABA in the presence of FUS3 was slower than that observed in the presence of ABI3. This suggested the involvement of a secondary ABA signaling mechanism and a difference in the mechanism underlying the interaction of ABA signaling with ABI3 or FUS3.

In summary, our kinetic analyses showed that regulation of seed storage protein gene promoters, particularly of CRC, by FUS3, and probably by ABI3 as well, not only involves their direct action, but also required the action of additional factors regulated by FUS3 and/or ABI3. Furthermore, we also demonstrated that FUS3 regulation of CRC expression was tightly coupled with ABA signaling. These findings are based on the ectopic induction of FUS3 in vegetative tissue. Although the mechanisms suggested by our ectopic expression system need to be confirmed in developing seeds, such processes would not have been easily uncovered without the ectopic induction system. The identity of the FUS3/ABA-regulated intermediate factor may be found through kinetic analyses using our FUS3 induction system and DNA microarrays covering most of the genome, and these experiments are now in progress.

Materials and Methods

Plasmid construction

Synthetic oligonucleotides with the following sequences were used as PCR primers to construct plasmids: M13–21, 5′-TGTAAAACGACGGCCAGT-3′; M13Rev, 5′-CAGGAAACAGCTATGACC-3′; FUS3-SalREV, 5′-GAGCGTCGACGTAGAAGTCATCGAGAGAG-3′; ABI3-SalREV, 5′-GCATGTCGACTTTAACAGTTTGAGAAGT-3′; T3, 5′-AATTAACCCTCACTAAAGGG-3′; F-dc1-j, 5′-ATAGATGATGTCACCACAAACTAGATAATCAATCGATGGCT-3′; R-dc1-j, 5′-AGCCCATCGATTGATTATCTAGTTTGTGGTGACATCATCTAT-3′; F-dc2-j, 5′-GAAGAAGTAGACGTAATCAATTAGATAATCAATCGATGGGCT-3′; R-dc2-j, 5′-AGCCCATCGATTGATTATCTAATTGATTACGTCTACTTCTTC-3′; F-dc3-j, 5′-GTCAGCTCTCTCCGACGTATGTAGATAATCAATCGATGGGCT-3′; R-dc3-j, 5′-AGCCCATCGATTGATTATCTACATACGTCGGAGAGAGCTGAC-3′; and dN-j, 5′-GGACTGCGGAATTCCACGTGCCAACTCCTCTCCCC-3′.

To obtain dHA-His-tagged expression vectors, the plasmid p35S-DH-NosT was constructed by inserting the CaMV 35S promoter, DH tag and Nos terminator sequences into pBluescript KS+. This plasmid contained the following restriction sites; 5′-KpnI–ApaI–XhoI–ClaI 5′ to the 35S promoter, 5′-EcoRI–SmaI–BamHI-SalI between the 35S promoter and DH sequence, PstI between the DH and Nos terminator sequences, and 5′-XbaI–NotI–SacII–SacI 3′ to the Nos terminator sequence (pBluescript-derived restriction sites are underlined). The DH sequence was 5′-GAATTCCCGGGGATCCGTCGACCCTTACCCATACGACGTTCCAGACTACGCTGGTTACCCATACGACGTTCCAGACTACGCTAGATCCGGTCACCACCACCACCACCACTAAGATCAATTCCTGCAG-3′, where the junction restriction sites mentioned above and termination codon are underlined and italicized, respectively.

To construct expression vectors for proteins tagged with the SV40 NLS and double myc epitope (dMyc), synthetic double-stranded DNA with the following sequence was inserted into the XhoI–EcoRI site of pBluescript (KS+) to create pNLS-dMyc; 5′- CTCGAGAAAATGATGCCACCAAAGAAGAAGAGAAAGGTTGGTGGTGACCCTGAACAAAAATTAATTTCTGAAGAAGATTTAGGTGAACAAAAATTAATTTCTGAAGAAGATTTAAGATCTGGTGGTGAATTC-3′, where the XhoI, BglII and EcoRI sites are underlined.

To construct expression vectors for proteins tagged with the SV40 NLS, dMyc and the VP16 activation domain, pNLS-dMyc-AD was created by inserting a BglII–EcoRI fragment of the VP16 activation domain into the corresponding sites in pNLS-dMyc.

For glucocorticoid- and Estr-inducible expression of FUS3, ABI3 or their derivatives in transgenic plants, Ti-plasmid binary vectors pTA7001 (Aoyama and Chua 1997) and pER8 (Zuo et al. 2000), respectively were used.

A FUS3 cDNA fragment containing the entire coding region was obtained by digesting an expressed sequence tag (EST) clone (AV561175) obtained from the Kazusa DNA Research Institute with XhoI, blunt ended by Klenow and cloned into the EcoRV site of pBluescript (KS+) (designated pFUS3RV#1). The FUS3 fragment was then excised with XhoI and SpeI, and cloned into the corresponding sites in pTA7001, resulting in pTA-FUS3.

The above-mentioned FUS3 cDNA XhoI fragment was directly cloned into the XhoI site of pBluescript KS+ (pFUS3Xho#2) and then amplified by PCR using primers M13–21 and FUS3-SalREV to remove the FUS3 termination codon and to add a SalI site. The PCR product was then cloned into pT7 Blue (Novagen) to generate pT-FUS3Salt#2. An EcoRI–BglII fragment excised from this plasmid was then inserted into pT-FUS3Salt#2 at the corresponding sites, generating pT-FUS3EBIIs#1, from which an XhoI–SalI fragment was excised and cloned into the SalI site of p35S-DH-NosT to generate p35S-FUS3-DH. The FUS3-DH fragment was excised with EcoRI–NotI, blunt ended with Klenow, and inserted into the EcoRV site of pBluescript SK+. The FUS3-DH fragment was then excised with XhoI and SpeI, and finally inserted into the corresponding sites of pTA7001, resulting in pTA-FUS3-DH.

An HpaI–SalI fragment of ABI3 cDNA clone pcabi3-4 (Giraudat et al. 1992) was blunt ended with Klenow and inserted into the EcoRV site of pBluescript (SK–) and a clone with the vector XhoI site at the 5′ side of the ABI3 sequence selected. An XhoI–SpeI fragment from this plasmid, designated pABI3HSfRV/SK#3, was inserted into the corresponding sites of TA7001, generating pTA-ABI3.

An ABI3 fragment amplified from pcabi3-4 by PCR using primers M13–21 and ABI3-SalREV, to remove the ABI3 termination codon and to add a SalI site, was cloned into pT7 Blue, resulting in pT-ABI3Salt#7. The SmaI–XhoI region of pT-ABI3Salt#7 was then replaced with the HpaI–XhoI ABI3 cDNA fragment to regain the original 5′ end. This plasmid, pT-ABI3HXs #7–1, was digested with SacI, blunt ended with T4 DNA polymerase, and digested with SalI. The resulting fragment was ligated into SmaI–SalI-digested p35S-DH-NosT, generating p35S-ABI3HXs-DH#7–1-1. The ABI3-dHA-His fragment was then excised with EcoRV and NotI, blunt ended with Klenow, and ligated into SpeI-digested blunt-ended pTA7001, to generate pTA-ABI3-DH.

The XhoI–SpeI fragment from pTA-FUS3 and pTA-FUS3-DH was cloned into the corresponding sites of pER8 to generate pER-FUS3 and pER-FUS3-DH, respectively.

EcoRI-digested pFUS3-RV#1 was blunt ended with Klenow and self-ligated, resulting in pFUS3 RV#1Ecof#1, which was used as PCR template. PCR products obtained using primer pairs M13 Rev and R-dC1-j, and M13–21 and F-dC1-j were diluted and mixed, and then used as a template in a PCR using the primer pair M13 Rev and M13–21 to amplify a C-terminally deleted form of FUS3 retaining the original 3′-untranslated region. The PCR product was then digested with XhoI and SpeI, cloned into the corresponding sites of pBluescript (SK+), and inserted into the XhoI–SpeI sites of pER8 to generate pER-FUS3dC1. pER-FUS3dC2 and pER-FUS3dC3 were generated similarly using primers F-dC2-j and R-dC2-j, and F-dC3-j and R-dC3-j, respectively, instead of R-dC1-j and F-dC1-j.

PCR products obtained using pFUS3RV#1Ecof #1 as template and primer pair M13–21 and dN-j were digested with EcoRI and SpeI and cloned into the corresponding sites of pBL-NLS-dMyc. From the resulting plasmid, an XhoI–SpeI fragment containing the NLS region and an N-terminal deletion of FUS3 was excised and inserted into the corresponding sites of pER8 to generate pER-NLS-FUS3dN.

PCR products obtained using pER-FUS3dC2 as template and the primer pair dN-j and M13–21 were digested with EcoRI and SpeI and cloned into the corresponding sites of pBL-NLS-dMyc or pBL-NLS-dMyc-AD to generate pER-NLS-B3 or pER-AD-B3, respectively.

Plant transformation

Plasmid constructs with pTA7001 or pER8 backbones were introduced into Agrobacterim tumefaciens GV3101 and then used to transform wild-type A. thaliana (ecotype Columbia) as described by Bechtold and Pelletier (1998) and Clough and Bent (1998). At least six lines selected from >30 transformed lines for each construct based on the transgene expression level and copy number were subjected to preliminary analysis. Because all of the selected lines showed consistent effects of the transgene induction on the SSP gene expression in the preliminary experiments, detailed analyses with one representative line for each construct were presented.

Plant treatments

Surface-sterilized homozygous transgenic Arabidopsis seeds were placed in 6-well cell culture plates (6-Well Cell Culture Cluster, Corning, NY, U.S.A.) containing 2.5 ml of liquid medium (half-strength MS salt, B5 vitamin, 1% sucrose), incubated for 24 h at 4°C, and then cultured with gentle shaking for 7 d at 22°C under continuous light. Plantlets were then treated with ABA, Dex or Estr at final concentrations of 50, 30 or 4 µM, respectively, or with combinations of ABA and Dex or Estr at the same concentrations.

RNA isolation and gel blot hybridization

Total RNA was isolated by the aurintricarboxylic acid (ATA) method as described by Wadsworth et al. (1988). RNA was fractionated by electrophoresis on formaldehyde-containing agarose gels and blotted onto nylon membranes (Hybond-XL, Amersham Pharmacia Biotech, Buckinghamshire, U.K.) by standard methods. RNA blots were hybridized in hybridization solution (250 mM Na2HPO4, pH 7.4, 7% SDS, 1 mM EDTA) containing 32P-labeled DNA probe at 65°C overnight, washed four times in wash buffer (40 mM Na2HPO4, pH 7.4, 1% SDS and 1 mM EDTA) and exposed to X-ray film or an IP plate. ABI3- and FUS3-specific probes consisted of an ∼600 bp PstI–XhoI fragment of pcabi3-4 or an ∼550 bp BglII–XhoI fragment of pFUS3-Xho#2, respectively. Other probes were prepared by PCR using either genomic DNA or the corresponding EST clone. PCR-amplified regions, as defined by nucleotide number relative to the putative translation start site, used were: CRC (At4G28520), 1180–1565; At2S3 (At4G27160), 16–519; AtEm1 (At3G51810), 147–458; AtEm6 (At2G40170), 1–276; ABI4 (At2g40220.1), –151 to 987; ABI5 (At2g36270), –23 to 556; LEC1 (Kazusa EST clone, SQ243a11F), –39 to 756; and AtbZIP67 (At3g44460.1), 541–807.
